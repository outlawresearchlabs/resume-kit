# Terminal Elegance

A design movement for documents that must live in two worlds: the machine-parsed and the human-admired.

Terminal Elegance is the quiet confidence of a perfectly configured shell rendered on paper. It rejects the lazy binary that "hacker" means dark mode and noise. Instead it takes the semiotics of the terminal - the prompt, the cursor, the monospace grid, the status bar, the circuit trace - and sets them on warm white paper with the restraint of Swiss typography. The result should read instantly as "this person lives in a terminal" to a practitioner, while reading as "this person is meticulous" to an executive. Every glyph earns its place. Every decoration is vector-drawn whitespace music, never clutter.

Color is a single voltage: phosphor green, stepped into three precise registers. A deep emerald (#047857) for text accents that must be read. A vivid signal green (#16C172) for marks that must be seen - the cursor block, the chevrons, the node terminals. A breath-light mint (#BBEAD3 rules, #F0FBF5 tints) that must only be felt. One drop of cyan (#22B8CF), used like a soldering point, no more. Ink is near-black with a cold cast (#0B1220). Everything else is paper.

Typography is a dialogue between two voices. The monospace voice (JetBrains Mono) speaks in prompts, paths, dates, and labels - it is the machine's voice, lowercase and exact, prefixed with `>` and `//` and `$`. The humanist voice (Instrument Sans) carries the prose. Hierarchy comes from weight, position, and the green register, not from size inflation. Headers sit on hairline rules that terminate in a single filled node, like a trace ending at a pad.

Space is the luxury. Margins are generous and constant. Sections breathe. The grid is felt, not seen - bullets hang from vector chevrons, category labels sit in a fixed column like keys in a config file, dates align right like timestamps in a log. Circuit traces occupy only the corners and only in whitespace. The document closes with `// end of file` and a cursor block, so the final page reads as intentional.

The artifact must survive two audiences with zero compromise: a parser can strip it to clean text in one pass (every decorative mark is vector, never a glyph), and a designer can stare at it and find nothing to fix.
